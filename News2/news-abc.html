How Australia could become the world's most COVID-19 vaccinated country, with 90 per cent coverage

By data journalist Catherine Hanrahan Posted Thu 26 Aug 2021 at 5:01pm, updated Thu 26 Aug 2021 at 11:43pm
Help keep family & friends informed by sharing this article

abc.net.au/news/how-australia-could-become-world-most-covid-vaccinated-country/100410504

Forget the other COVID-19 inoculation targets — one of Australia's top public health officials wants to see at least 90 per cent of us get the jab and become "the most vaccinated country in the world".

And, experts say if we "throw everything at it", it's possible.

Australians living under lockdown have been told they'll have more freedoms when 70 per cent and 80 per cent of the adult population are fully vaccinated.

As the number of new COVID-19 infections in NSW topped 1,000 on Thursday, Chief Health Officer Kerry Chant used the milestone to push for a bigger target.

"As a public health doctor, I want to help my ED colleagues and my dream is to have us be the most vaccinated country in the world," she said.

"For me, the sky is the limit and I think Australians can do it."

If Australia achieved that goal, it would match the most highly vaccinated countries in the world.

Global vaccination rates are compared across the whole population of each country.

Vaccinating 90 per cent of the adult population aged 16 years and over who are currently eligible, equates to 72 per cent of the entire population.

Only four countries in the world have so far achieved that level.

Julie Leask, a vaccine uptake expert from the school of nursing and midwifery at the University of Sydney, said governments would need a highly targeted push to get that many people inoculated.

"It's possible but we'd have to throw everything at it," she said.

She said governments would need to understand why people were not getting vaccinated.

"It starts with collaborating with the community and that's very resource intensive and time-consuming," she said.

"So if we want to get to 90 per cent, we may need more of that style of a tailoring approach."

She said the evidence showed that the rate started to plateau in other countries once they reached 70 per cent.

"When supply exceeds demand, you start to have to reach the people who are more hesitant, or more poorly reached by usual systems," she said.

Malta tops the world with 80 per cent of its population vaccinated.

While it has a higher proportion of its population aged over 16 than Australia, it also bought 2 million doses of vaccine , enough to vaccinate the population twice over.

It then distributed doses via community health centres.

"That number could drop if these are the people who were unsure about AstraZeneca, but were comfortable with moving forward with another option, then we may get some of those people across, because they talk about safety," she said.

Professor Leask would like to see programs to reach cultural groups as has been done in Sydney's south-west, particularly those who are socially disconnected from mainstream society.

"We know how infectious Delta is and it will rip through any under-vaccinated areas," she said.

She said NSW Health wanted to see homeless people, as well as those battling drug and alcohol addiction, and living with mental illness to be priorities, as well as people in prisons.
